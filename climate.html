Which months are the CO2 values at the maximum? Minimum? Why is this?

  • CO2 is maximally released during summer months and minimally released during winter months. This is possibly attributed to plant growth cycles; during summer months plants have heavy growtjh and are actively photosynthesizing which removes co2 from the atmosphere. During winter months however, plants are often dead or idle so they are not removing noticable amounts of co2 form the atmosphere.

What rolling average is used in computing the "trend" line? How does the trend depend on the rolling average?

  • This rolling average should be (and is) a simple rolling average that taeks every value at equal weight and computes the average per unit over time.

Question 1:

Describe the data set to the best of your ability given the documentation provided. Describe what kind of column each data contains and what units it is measured in. Then address our three key questions in understanding this data:

The data shows three columns: year, no_smoothing, and lowess(5). It tells us the temperature anomolies, and how drastic they were, over time. The numbers in the lowess columns are a statistically manipulated value that locally weights the scatter plot in order to smooth the data and show a more general trend by averaging short term drastic fluctuations. This data is recorded in celsius.

  • How are the measurements made? What is the associated measurement uncertainty?

  • This data is collected from a range of scientific instruments, such as sattelite data, weather balloon, ships, and meteroligcal stations across the world. The measurement uncertainty therefore comes from possible faulty instrument percision, and graps across areas of the world where sampling may have been less.

  • What is the resolution of the data?

  • The temporal resolution is one year, and the spatial resolution is global.

  • Are their missing values? How should they be handled?

  • There are no obviously visible missing values from this data set, but if there were they should be removal (leaving the bad data out of the analysis) or use imputation to accurately assume what the data point should be in according to the data points around it.

  • The 'TIME' column is measured in decimal year (2002.29 = 29% into 2002) in order to have a more accurate account for when the data was collected. Exercise III: Rising Sea Levels?

  • Data description: http://climate.nasa.gov/vital-signs/sea-level/
  • Raw data file: http://climate.nasa.gov/system/internal_resources/details/original/121_Global_Sea_Level_Data_File.txt

Question 1:

  • Describe the data set: what are the columns and units?
  • The columns are:
  • altimeter_type, which is the tyoe of sattelite altimeter used to collect data.
    cycle_number, which is the number of orbit cycles that sattelite has completed when the data is collected
    year_fraction, year in decimal form for increased time accuracy
    num_observations, number of obseravtions completed by sattelite
    num_weighted_observations, weighted (to quality) number of obseravtions completed by sattelite GMSL_variation_GIA_not_applied, variation in Global Mean Sea Level (GMSL) without correction for Glacial Isostatic Adjustment (GIA, which accounts for earths rebound after melting)
    std_dev_GMSL_GIA_not_applied, std deviation of variation in GMSL without accounting for GIA
    smoothed_GMSL_GIA_not_applied, smoothed (short term flucuations) GMSL variation without GIA
    GMSL_variation_GIA_applied, variation of GMSL accounting for GIA
    std_dev_GMSL_GIA_applied, std deviation of variation in GMSL accounting for GIA smoothed_GMSL_GIA_applied, smoothed (short term flucuations) GMSL variation accounting for GIA smoothed_GMSL_annual_semi_annual_removed, smoothed GSML variations with smoothed annual fluctions (summer/winter)
  • Where do these data come from?
  • satellite altimetry measurements of global sea level, this data specifically comes form NASAs Goddard Space Flight Center.
  • What is the uncertainty in measurment? Resolution of the data? Interpretation of missing values?
  • Measurement uncertainty can come from instrumental uncertainty when its takin gmeasurmenets, uncertainty from an over/undercorrection from the GIA, and statistical error in data smoothing. The temporal resolution is sub annual , and the spatial resolution is large scale, global oceans.

Exercise IV: Arctic Sea Ice?

  • http://nsidc.org/data/G02135
  • https://noaadata.apps.nsidc.org/NOAA/G02135/north/monthly/data/

Question 1:

  • Describe the data set: what are the columns and units?
  • The columns are: year, month, data type, region, extent, and area. The units are year, month, data type = categorical (Goddard Space Flight Center), region = categroical (northern alantic region), extent = Million square kilometers (M km)(total area where sea ice concentration is at least 15%.), area = Million square kilometers (M km), (actual area of ocean covered by sea ice)
  • Where do these data come from?
  • This data comes from measurements taken by NASA's Goddard Space Flight Center.
  • What is the uncertainty in measurement? Resolution of the data? Interpretation of missing values?
  • Missing or bad value flag: 99900.000, so these values are removed. Temporal resolution is per month of each year, and spatial resolution of satelites is usually 10-20km in the northern alantic region. The uncertainty could come from machine mistake (can be caused by weather, etc.) or data processing mistakes.
